## Problem Description

The following are four excerpts from Old Norse poems composed around 900 C.E., written using the meter named **dróttkvætt** (lit. 'court meter'):

### Excerpt I
```
1 ók at ísarnleiki
2 Jarðar sunr, en dunði ...
```

### Excerpt II
```
1 þekkiligr með þegnum
2 þrymseilar hval deila.
3 en af breiðu bjóði
4 bragðvíss at þat lagði
5 ósvífrandi ása
6 upp þjórhluti fjóra.
```

### Excerpt III
```
1 áðr gnapsólar Gripnis
2 gnýstœrandi fœri
3 rausnarsamr til rimmu
4 ríðviggs lagar skíðum.
```

### Excerpt IV
```
1 háði gramr, þars gnúðu,
2 geira hregg við seggi,
3 (rauð fnýsti ben blóði)
4 bryngo˛gl í dyn Sko˛glar,
5 þás á rausn fyr ræsi
6 (réð egglituðr) seggir ...
```

### Part A
One of the main principles of dróttkvætt is **alliteration**. The first line of each distich (pair of lines) contains two words beginning with the same sound, and the first word of the second line begins with this sound, too.

Example: *rausnarsamr*, *rimmu* and *ríðviggs* (III:3–4)

All vowels are considered to alliterate with one another and with *j*.

Example: *ók*, *ísarnleiki* and *Jarðar* (I:1–2)

**But this is not the only rule.**

Describe the rules which are observed in a distich of dróttkvætt.

### Part B
Given is a stanza in which 13 words are omitted:

```

2  b        ; kno˛´ttu spjo˛´r  c      )
```

The following list contains (in alphabetical order) all 13 omitted words and two words which do not belong in stanza V:

Fill in the gaps in stanza V.

## Notes

### Phonetic Notes
- æ ≈ English *a* in "cat"
- œ = French *eu* or German *ö* (long vowels)
- ø = short œ
- y = French *u* or German *ü*
- o˛ = open o
- au and ei are pronounced as single syllables
- ð and þ = English *th* in "this" and "thin" respectively
- x = k+s
- The mark ´ denotes vowel length

All samples of poetry conform to the rules of the genre.

## Linguistic Insights

### Key Patterns to Discover

1. **Alliteration Patterns**:
   - Lines 1-2 of each distich follow specific alliteration rules
   - Two stressed words in line 1 alliterate
   - First stressed word in line 2 alliterates with them
   - Vowels alliterate with any vowel or *j*

2. **Additional Dróttkvætt Rules**:
   - **Syllable count**: Each line typically has 6 syllables
   - **Internal rhyme**: Lines contain internal rhymes (skothending/aðalhending)
   - **Stress pattern**: Specific stress patterns required
   - **Line pairs**: Odd-numbered lines use one rhyme type, even use another

3. **Rhyme Types**:
   - **Aðalhending** (full rhyme): Matching vowels and following consonants
   - **Skothending** (half rhyme): Matching consonants only

4. **Word Selection Criteria** for Part B:
   - Must satisfy alliteration rules
   - Must fit metrical pattern (syllable count)
   - Must satisfy internal rhyme schemes
   - Must be grammatically/semantically appropriate

### Solution Strategy

1. **Part A - Rule Discovery**:
   - Analyze all given examples for alliteration
   - Count syllables per line
   - Identify internal rhyme positions
   - Find rhyme types (vowel+consonant vs consonant only)
   - Note stress positions

2. **Part B - Gap Filling**:
   - Determine required alliteration for each position
   - Check syllable count constraints
   - Match internal rhyme requirements
   - Eliminate words that don't fit phonologically
   - Use semantic context as final check

### Specific Analysis

**Alliteration Examples**:
- I:1-2: *ók*, *ísarnleiki* | *Jarðar* (all vowels)

## Cultural Context

Dróttkvætt was the prestige meter of Old Norse court poetry, used by skalds (court poets) to praise kings and warriors. Its extreme complexity made it a mark of poetic skill and learning.
